What is an LLM?

  • Traditional AI: Data → Label → Train → Predict
  • Example: Risk model for company defaults
  • LLMs: A specific type of model...

So, What is an LLM? How are they created?

  • The high-level process of creating an A.I. Model is straightforward:
    • You need data
    • Label that data
    • Train a model on that data
    • Use the model to make predictions on similar cases
  • Example: To create a risk model predicting company defaults:
    • Gather information about existing companies (revenue, costs, size, etc.)
    • Label which companies went into default and which did not
    • Train a model on this dataset
    • Use the model to produce risk scores for future companies
  • This is called supervised training. The more data you have, the better your model performs.
  • Getting labeled data is expensive:
    • If it costs 5 cents to label one record, 1 million records cost $50,000
    • Double-checking each record would cost $100,000
  • An LLM is a specific model trained on natural language sources, which allows for unsupervised training.
  • You do not have to label the data; labels are inferred from the training data itself.
  • Text is everywhere, making it easy to gather enormous datasets for training.
  • With access to vast data (like the entire public internet) and the resources for unsupervised training, we can now create large language models.
  • ChatGPT, for example, is an LLM trained on a diverse range of internet text.

Based on Probability

Tuning Parameters
  • So what goes on under the hood? Most of the models we use today are called "next token generators." Given an input, they use probabilistic calculations to generate an output.
  • What does that mean? For example, if I ask you your favorite world cuisine, you might answer "Vietnamese." If I ask you again a minute later, you'll likely give the same answer. A model, however, might give a different answer the second time, because its responses are based on probability, not certainty.
  • The nature of these models is probabilistic, not deterministic. That's why it's impossible to guarantee 100% predictable answers. There are techniques to improve consistency, but it's important to understand that this technology is fundamentally based on probability calculations.
  • If the models were always completely random, they would be useless. Fortunately, there are parameters you can adjust, such as "top-p" and "temperature," to control the randomness. (Demo these settings.)
  • DEMO: This will be more clear when we look at a small example. The temperature adjusts the probability distribution through a mathematical function. When the temperature is low, the probability gap between words widen, favoring the high probability words. When the temperature is high, the probability distribution becomes more even, giving lower probability words a better chance of being selected.
  • Top-P acts like a filter or a window. When the value is high, the window size is larger, so allow more words to come in, to be selected as the next word, and vice versa, when it's low and it's narrow, so less words will get in.

Capabilities

LLMs excel where both the input and output are words, and the rules are fuzzy.

  • Text generation and completion
  • Language translation
  • Code generation and debugging
  • Question answering and reasoning
  • Creative writing and content creation
  • Data analysis and summarization
  • Now you have a high-level idea of how LLMs work under the hood—they're probability machines. Because of their immense size, they can seemingly do everything.
  • But what are they good at? And what are they not good at? Let's explore that for a bit…
  • LLMs excel where both the input and output are words, and the rules are fuzzy.
  • But they can also do images right? Right, but they use different models for that. When you ask ChatGPT to generate an image, it's actually using a separate model trained on visual data.
  • To keeps things simple, we'll stick to Language models.
    • Think of an LLM like that one friend who’s read everything, speaks eloquently, and will confidently answer anything you ask… but sometimes makes things up without realizing it. The trick is knowing when to trust that friend, and when to check the facts.
  • Some very successful AI tools include:
    • Translators, even real-time translation
    • Note-taking and meeting summarization (e.g., NoteBookLM)

Limitations

LLMs struggle with precision, real-time data, and tasks requiring exact logic.

  • Hallucinations - making up facts confidently
  • Mathematical calculations and precise logic
  • No access to real-time information
  • Consistent personality or memory across sessions
  • Understanding of physical world constraints
  • Inability to learn from individual conversations
    • LLMs are not calculators or truth machines.
    • They do not know facts; they predict likely answers based on patterns in data.
    • LLMs are excellent storytellers, but not always reliable truth-tellers. This can be hard to get used to.
    • We are used to computers giving precise, correct answers—like calculators in math class. If a calculator gives a wrong answer, it's a bug!
    • With LLMs, for the first time, we must accept that a computer might be wrong or uncertain.
    • Older LLMs (like GPT-2) might confidently answer questions like "What time is it?" with a random, incorrect time.
    • LLMs understand your question and generate a syntactically correct answer, but it may not be factually accurate.
    • modern models seem to have overcome these limitations, but actually, many of them are not solved by creating better models, but by system prompts and software around the model.

  • Now let's talk about how to actually build AI into your applications. There are several architectural approaches, and I always recommend: START SIMPLE.

  • The key takeaway: Start with a simple prompt, then add complexity only when you need it. Don't build a space shuttle when a bicycle will do the job.

  • Role: Define a perspective from where AI should act as (e.g., expert, assistant, teacher).

  • So we have a basic structure. Now, where there is structure, there are also patterns. I'll quickly dive into a few of most well known ones: Few shots, Chain of Thought, and Flipped interaction.
  • Few-Shot Pattern: This is like teaching by demonstration — instead of telling the model the rule, you just show it a couple of examples.
  • Works well when you need consistent formatting or when you're nudging the model toward a specific style.
  • Example: give a few Q&A pairs in your desired tone, and the model will continue in the same style.
  • Chain of Thought: Back in 2023, long ago in A.I. terms, they were searching for how to unlock reasoning capabilities in these models.
  • So, they found that by just telling the model to "think step by step" - by explicitly asking the model to reason step by step, you reduce errors in logic-heavy tasks.
  • Great for debugging code, solving word problems, or explaining technical reasoning. It makes the thought process visible — which also makes it easier for humans to catch mistakes.
  • This is also built in, in the system prompts of modern models. When you ask GPT 5 a question requiring multiple steps to answer, you can see it working through the problem step by step.
  • Flipped Interaction: Another great example of a prompting pattern is Flipped interaction. When prompting, you're probably used to asking questions and getting answers, right?
  • But what if you don't know the questions? What if you need an answer in a domain you know nothing about?
  • Instead of jumping straight to an answer, the model plays back like a consultant: "Wait, I need more info first."
  • Very effective in domains you do not know much about.
  • This is also one of the patterns which is built into the system prompts of larger models. Anyone of you ever tried Deep Research? For instance from ChatGPT? You can ask it a question and it will spend like 5-20 minutes researching, reasoning and compiling an answer.
  • If you ask it a short question, it will almost always reply by asking you return questions. Because deep research takes a lot of time from the model, and time for you, and costs way more tokens than a regular question. So it would be a waste if it didn't understand your question correctly, so it will try to get it clear.

Prompts in your application

  • Prompts are the way to communicate with an LLM. But what not everyone realizes yet is that prompts are THE way to communicate with an LLM. There is no other. So if you build an application which uses an LLM to perform a task, you need a prompt, natural language, in your software. The prompt contains the question you want the LLM to answer, so it will contain business logic. Prompts in your software are called system prompts.
  • Some system prompts enrich user input for instance in a chatbot. Some system prompts are completely invisible to the user, for instance, when your app does not have direct user interface. For example, we have an app, analysing financial documents.
  • Used in this way, prompts are not so different from the source code of your application. My advice here is to treat it as such.

Risks

Three main types of prompt attacks to defend against:

  • Prompt Extraction
    Extracting the application's system prompt to replicate or exploit
  • Jailbreaking & Prompt Injection
    Getting the model to perform harmful or unintended actions
  • Information Extraction
    Getting the model to reveal training data or context information
  • Every technoRisks you should be aware of when you build an application which harnesses the power of AI. There are 3 main types of prompt attacks you want to defend against:
  • Prompt extraction - Extracting the application's prompt, including the system prompt, either to replicate or exploit the application
  • Jailbreaking and prompt injection - Getting the model to do bad things
  • Information extraction - Getting the model to reveal its training data or information used in its context

Possible Consequences

  • Remote Code or Tool Execution
    Unauthorized execution of code or system tools
  • Data Leaks
    Extraction of private system and user information
  • Social Harms
    Providing knowledge for dangerous or criminal activities
  • Misinformation
    Manipulation of model outputs to spread false information
  • Service Interruption and Subversion
    Unauthorized access, incorrect decisions, or service disruption
  • Brand Risk
    Toxic or inappropriate statements causing PR crises
  • Prompt attacks pose multiple risks for applications; some are more devastating than others. Here are just a few of them:
  • Remote code or tool execution - For applications with access to powerful tools, bad actors can invoke unauthorized code or tool execution. Imagine if someone finds a way to get your system to execute an SQL query that reveals all your users' sensitive data or sends unauthorized emails to your customers. As another example, let's say you use AI to help you run a research experiment, which involves generating experiment code and executing that code on your computer. An attacker can find ways to get the model to generate malicious code to compromise your system.
  • Data leaks - Bad actors can extract private information about your system and your users.
  • Social harms - AI models help attackers gain knowledge and tutorials about dangerous or criminal activities, such as making weapons, evading taxes, and exfiltrating personal information.
  • Misinformation - Attackers might manipulate models to output misinformation to support their agenda.
  • Service interruption and subversion - This includes giving access to a user who shouldn't have access, giving high scores to bad submissions, or rejecting a loan application that should've been approved. A malicious instruction that asks the model to refuse to answer all the questions can cause service interruption.
  • Brand risk - Having politically incorrect and toxic statements next to your logo can cause a PR crisis, such as when Google AI search urged users to eat rocks or when Microsoft's chatbot Tay spat out racist comments. Even though people might understand that it's not your intention to make your application offensive, they can still attribute the offenses to your lack of care about safety or just incompetence.

The Good and Bad

  • ▸ Tech lead skills increasingly in demand
  • ▸ Testing & validation expertise critical
  • ▸ Architecture decisions more important than ever
  • ▸ Reliability, performance, security skills highly prized
  • ▸ Non-technical people can now prototype apps
  • ▸ Language specialization becoming less valuable
  • ▸ Frontend/backend roles increasingly blurred
  • ▸ Routine implementation work automated by AI
  • The Good: AI shifts developer focus to higher-value work
  • Tech lead skills: Writing good requirements becomes crucial - someone needs to tell AI what to build
  • Testing: Can't trust AI-generated code without proper test coverage and CI/CD infrastructure
  • Architecture: More code generated faster means structure and boundaries are critical
  • Quality skills: Reliability, performance, security expertise is what separates engineers from "coders"
  • The Bad: Traditional developer skills declining in value
  • Prototyping democratized: Platforms like Replit/Lovable let anyone build apps (Shaq coded an app!)
  • Language expertise: Being a Go or Rust expert matters less when AI can write any language
  • Specialization erosion: Less need for separate frontend/backend devs when AI bridges the gap
  • Routine work automated: Well-defined tickets (bugs, small features) increasingly handled by AI
  • Key message: Shift from coding to engineering - implementation skills less valuable, design & validation skills more valuable
